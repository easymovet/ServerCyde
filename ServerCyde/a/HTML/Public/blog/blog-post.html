We got hit by a COMET!

Aug 7 2011
Posted in Uncategorized

While working building out the JS library to allow secure client side connections to Amazon SimpleDB we thought of adding a COMET service! It sounded like so much fun to build that we got side tracked and while the DB implementation is at about 50% the COMET implementation is done!

There are only 2 methods needed to harness COMET on our site: Subscribe and Publish. You subscribe to a channel using this line from the browser:
ServerCyde.Comet.Subscribe(1, function (data) { alert("message:" + data); });
then when you publish something anyone who is connected to channel 1 will get the published message INSTANTLY, no repeated polling required. Publishing a message is just as easy:
ServerCyde.Comet.Publish(1, "New person online!", null, function (data) { });

We are pretty close to releasing a beta. Email templates are almost done, you will be able to add information for an SMTP server of your choosing like SendGrid (we use PostMark) and setup some templates that you can trigger a send from the client using only JS. That's right, you will be sending emails using only JS!

As always we love your feedback on features, or just plain excitement :)

-Riaz ServerCyde

Notes from a bootstrapper's breakfast

Jul 27 2011
Posted in Uncategorized

I just got back from my first bootstrapper's breakfast here in Chicago. It was a great event to meet entrepreneurs who all have similar challenges. The moderator at my table was very knowledgeable and was very willing to share his expertise. So what was the focus? There were three main points: 1) how to reach your customers organically on the cheap; 2) how to reward early partners; 3) how to set your price.

1) How to your reach your customers organically on the cheap. The main consensus at our table was to become a thought leader in your area. There are two main ways to do that. The first is to ask thought provoking questions of your customers and peers. This strategy allows you to build a growing list of individuals who will read your question, see if it applies to their situation, and answer it from their perspective. Second, you should be constantly looking for questions that need quality answers and answer those in which you can provide such a substantive answer. Both of these strategies should be used without pushing your product and both require an active presence on particular websites, LinkedIn was suggested. It is a hard task, of course, to talk amongst your target market without pushing your product. However, in the long term, a certain amount of equity is built in the organic conversations between you and your potential customers.

Speaking of equity, 2) how to reward early partners or employees. Going into this breakfast it was my understanding that at the earliest stage of a bootstrapped startup, equity is really the only path. While still an important consideration, there is also the option of a promised share of profits. Additionally, if equity is what's on the table, it is vital to get the terms in writing. Get it in writing even if the relationship between you and the partner is good now; think of it as insurance for that relationship. Some good things to have is a three or four year vesting period with a one year “cliff”. A “cliff” means that the partner is basically on probation for the first year, gaining no vested interest until after completing the first year. But a vested interested interest is no good unless you're making money.

3) How to set your price. We touched on this last point only briefly and this seemed to be taken not so seriously. One suggestion was just to ask your customers what their willingness-to-pay is for the service. However, given my exposure to willingness-to-pay in economics, I have a somewhat cynical view of this; one's stated willingness-to-pay is not exactly what they actually will pay. Another idea was to be under the threshold of the so-called expense or approval line. That is, charge only enough so the customer does not have to seek approval to pay for your service and that they can simply expense it on their company card. Finally, a bit of cynicism from the older people at my table came through when they brought up doing a market analysis and suggesting that maybe there is no market and you should just give up. I don't think that is the best advice, and I surely will not be heeding it.

All in all, I learned a bunch from this breakfast. It was $5 to attend and the food was pretty cheap. Even if I did not learn any of the above things, the opportunity to talk about my start up in front of other people was worth the cost of attendance. I have another event that I am going to tomorrow night where I am to give a short presentation, so the extra practice this breakfast gave me was invaluable. Hope to see you at the next bootstrapper's breakfast.

- Amir

Startup Web App Development Architecture

Jun 27 2011
Posted in Uncategorized

When turning a business idea into reality the major considerations are cost, time to market, and scaling strategy. Cost is key. If you have a lot of money, then by some entrepreneurial standards you've already succeeded. If you are boot strapping like the rest of us, then you want your product to go live - yesterday. You want something to pitch to potential investors before the competition beats you to it. That's where ServerCyde comes in. Instead of hiring n-tier developers, you simply need a designer who knows JavaScript to use ServerCyde tools. Using ServerCyde will save designers from spending time building boilerplate code and allow them to manage things like sign-ups, Facebook connect, and data storage.

Building an app entirely in JavaScript has the secondary advantage of putting most of the processing task on the client. This scales very well because every new client is also a new "server." In essence, it scales on it's own. Building your app in HTML/CSS/JavaScript and using ServerCyde is particularly successful because it works on every platform and is future-proof. The ServerCyde platform is currently adding services to handle image uploads and manipulation, encryption, and email templates that will let you build rich apps all on the client.

Have you ever tried pitching a website to someone without having a prototype? Image pitching Twitter would go something like this: "You can only post 140 letters and other people are notified when you make a new post.” Without seeing the end product most people would think the idea is terrible. After all, who would want to read just 140 letters without useful content? With no prototype, even the best idea is a difficult sell.
